Galvanized vs. poly: which tank is right for you?

Galvanized steel tanks with a food-grade liner are the classic Big Island choice — they look at home in Hawaiian Paradise Park, handle our weather well, and can last 30 years or more with care. Poly (polyethylene) tanks cost less up front, install faster, and resist rust entirely, which some homeowners prefer. We'll walk your property and recommend the option that fits your budget and your site honestly — no pressure either way.

Why Puna systems need to be built right

In Hawaiian Paradise Park, Pahoa, and across Puna, your roof is your water source — and the climate is demanding. With 120+ inches of rain a year, VOG drifting in from Kīlauea, and constant organic debris off the trees, a catchment system here works harder than almost anything on the mainland. A tank that's undersized, sitting on a poor base, or missing real filtration will give you trouble for years.

That's why we size for your actual rainfall, prep the base properly, and never skip the sediment, carbon, and UV stages. Built right the first time, your system is something you can stop worrying about.

Common questions

What size tank do I need?

For most Puna households a 10,000–12,000 gallon tank gives a comfortable buffer through dry spells. We size yours from your roof area, rainfall, and how many people the home supports — never a one-size guess.

Do I need a permit?

Requirements depend on your parcel and setup. We're familiar with what East Hawaii expects and will guide you through any permitting and timeline as part of the project.

Is catchment water safe to drink?

Yes — with a properly built tank and multi-stage filtration (sediment, carbon, UV), catchment water is safe for drinking, cooking, and all household use. We build every system to that standard.
