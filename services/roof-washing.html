Why your roof is your water's first filter

In Hawaiian Paradise Park, Pahoa, and across Puna, rain hits your roof and runs straight to your tank. Whatever is sitting on that roof — VOG drifting in from Kīlauea, moss feeding on 120+ inches of annual rain, bird droppings, and constant organic debris — washes into your catchment with the next storm. The cleaner your roof, the less of that load your filters and tank ever have to deal with.

That's also why the washing method matters so much. A standard roof wash often uses bleach or detergents that are fine for a county-water home but a real problem for catchment — those chemicals follow the same path the rain does, right into your tank. We use methods chosen to clean effectively without contaminating your stored water.

Common questions

Will washing my roof contaminate my catchment water?

Not the way we do it. We deliberately avoid the bleach and detergents used in standard roof washing, because on catchment those chemicals run straight into your tank. Our method cleans without putting anything harmful in your water.

How often should a catchment roof be washed?

It depends on your tree cover and VOG exposure, but most Puna roofs benefit from a wash every year or two. If you can see moss or a gray film building up, it's time.

Does a clean roof really make my water better?

Yes. Less buildup on the roof means less sediment and organic load reaching your tank, which keeps your water clearer, your filters lasting longer, and your tank cleaner between deep cleans.
