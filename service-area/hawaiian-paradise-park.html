Catchment is a way of life in HPP

Hawaiian Paradise Park is one of the largest subdivisions in the state, spread across thousands of lots between Kea'au and the coast. There's no municipal water line out here — essentially every household collects its own rain off the roof and stores it in a catchment tank. That makes a healthy system more than a convenience; it's how your ʻohana drinks, cooks, and bathes.

Because we live and work in the Park, we know the local quirks: the long gravel avenues, the older redwood and corrugated tanks still in service, and the heavy Puna rain that fills a tank fast but carries plenty of leaf litter and debris with it.

Why HPP tanks need regular care

Puna gets well over 100 inches of rain a year, and HPP sits right in the thick of it. All that water is a blessing, but it also washes roof grit, pollen, VOG fallout, and organic debris straight into your tank. Sediment settles on the bottom, biofilm grows on the walls, and over a few years that buildup becomes the perfect home for bacteria — even with good filtration downstream.

A clean, well-maintained catchment system means better-tasting water, longer-lasting filters, and a pump that doesn't have to work overtime. Living this far off-grid, your water system is one thing you don't want to neglect.

Common questions from HPP neighbors

Do you really serve all of Hawaiian Paradise Park?

Yes — HPP is our home base. From the makai avenues near the highway to the lots up toward Kea'au, we cover the whole subdivision and can usually get to you quickly.

My HPP home is entirely on catchment — is that water safe to drink?

It is, with a clean tank and proper multi-stage filtration (sediment, carbon, UV). Most issues we see in the Park come down to a tank that's overdue for cleaning or filters past their change date.

How fast can you get to my lot?

Because we're local to the Park, we can often schedule an HPP assessment within a day or two — and same-day for true emergencies like a major leak.
