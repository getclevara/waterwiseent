Catchment up here works harder

Mountain View sits at a higher elevation than the coastal Puna towns, and that changes everything about your water. It's cooler, it's wetter, and the rain rarely lets up for long. That heavy rainfall is a blessing — your tank stays full even through a dry spell — but it also means more roof runoff, more organic debris from the canopy, and faster sediment buildup at the bottom of the tank.

On the large rural lots common up the mauka side of Highway 11, your catchment system is often your only water source. There's no county line to fall back on if something goes wrong. That's exactly why regular cleaning and maintenance matter more here than almost anywhere on the island.

Why heavy rainfall means more maintenance

  • More runoff, more debris — constant rain washes leaves, pollen, and grit off your roof and straight into the tank.
  • Faster sediment buildup — the more water cycles through, the quicker the sludge layer grows on the tank floor.
  • Damp, shaded roofs — cooler, wetter conditions grow moss and algae that need regular roof washing to keep your water clean.
  • Filters work overtime — high-volume systems burn through sediment and carbon stages faster, so they need checking more often.

Common questions from Mountain View neighbors

Do you really come all the way up to Mountain View?

We do — Mountain View and upper Puna along Highway 11 are part of our regular service area. The drive is no problem; we plan routes so your visit is on schedule.

My tank fills so fast — do I still need cleaning?

Yes, and often sooner. A fast-filling tank cycles more water and debris through, so sediment builds quicker. We usually recommend a deep clean every 2–3 years up here, sometimes sooner.

Is the heavy rain bad for my catchment?

Not at all — it keeps you full. It just means roof washing and filter changes matter more, since more runoff carries more for your system to handle.
